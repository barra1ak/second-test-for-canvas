Scenario 1: Input Cost Shock

Situation: Fertilizer prices jumped 20% after global supply concerns. You need to decide whether to buy now, split the purchase, or wait.

Financial Context

You have $250,000 in operating cash. Fertilizer will likely cost $35,000–$48,000 depending on timing.

Strategic Tension

Buying now protects against further price increases, but reduces cash available for fuel, labor, and repairs.

Operational Risk

Under-applying fertilizer may save money short term, but could reduce yield and revenue at harvest.

Business Lesson

In agriculture, “saving money” is not always the same as protecting profitability.

The agronomist warns that reducing fertilizer too aggressively could lower yield potential, especially if weather conditions are favorable later.
The CFO recommends protecting cash flow, but says a partial lock-in may reduce exposure without draining liquidity.
Input costs are expenses required to produce a crop: seed, fertilizer, crop protection, fuel, labor, repairs, financing, and equipment.
Scenario 2: Weather Delay

Situation: Heavy rain delays planting by two weeks. The team is behind schedule and equipment/labor costs are rising.

Financial Context

Every delay compresses the season. More work has to happen in less time, often increasing overtime, repairs, and fuel costs.

Revenue Risk

Late planting may reduce yield potential. Lower yield means lower revenue even if market prices are strong.

Decision Pressure

You can spend more to stay on schedule, cut costs, or switch plans entirely.

Business Lesson

Timing is a financial variable in agriculture. A calendar problem can quickly become a margin problem.

Operations recommends overtime and equipment coordination to protect the planting window, even though it increases near-term costs.
Sales notes that customers and partners often underestimate how quickly weather changes financial plans.
Yield risk is the possibility that crop output will fall below expectations, reducing revenue and profitability.
Scenario 3: Market Opportunity

Situation: Corn prices rise before harvest. You can lock in a price, sell later, or store grain and wait.

Financial Context

Higher prices create opportunity, but prices can reverse quickly based on supply, demand, weather, and export news.

Cash Flow Need

You still need to cover harvest expenses, loan payments, repairs, and year-end obligations.

Storage Tradeoff

Storing grain can capture higher prices later, but storage, shrink, quality risk, and interest costs reduce gains.

Business Lesson

Risk management is not about predicting perfectly. It is about protecting the business from bad outcomes.

The grain marketer recommends locking in a portion of the crop to protect profit while leaving some upside open.
The banker likes decisions that improve certainty because predictable cash flow supports repayment and future borrowing.
Hedging is a way to reduce price risk by locking in or protecting a price for part of expected production.
